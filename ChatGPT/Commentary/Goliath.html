Goliath (Golyat – possibly “Exile” or “Splendor”)

Goliath was a Philistine (Pelishti – Immigrant) warrior from Gath (Gat – Winepress), described in 1 Samuel 17 as a fearsome giant and champion. His name’s exact meaning is uncertain but may relate to “exile” or “splendor.”

Physical Description

  • Height: Six cubits and a span (approximately 9 feet 9 inches / 3 meters), though some ancient texts say 4 cubits and a span (6’9”).
  • Wore bronze armor weighing about 125–150 pounds (57–68 kg).
  • Spear shaft was like a weaver’s beam; iron point weighed about 15–18 pounds (7–8 kg).

Role in the Battle

Goliath challenged Israel to single combat and mocked the God (Elohim – Mighty Ones) of Israel. He was defeated by David (David – Beloved), a young shepherd who struck him in the forehead with a stone from a sling and then cut off his head with Goliath’s own sword.

Legacy

  • Symbol of seemingly invincible enemies.
  • "David vs. Goliath" has become a universal metaphor for underdog victory.

Goliath’s Brothers and Kin

According to 2 Samuel 21:15–22 and 1 Chronicles 20:4–8, Goliath had brothers or close relatives who were also giants:

Conclusion

Goliath was not alone—he was part of a warrior class of giants rooted in the Philistine city of Gath and descended from the Rephaim. His defeat by David began a series of victories by David and his men over these giants, demonstrating divine support for Israel’s king and people.
