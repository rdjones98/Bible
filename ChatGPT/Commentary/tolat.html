Psalm 22:6 – The Meaning of "Worm" (Tola'at)

1. Literal Meaning

The Hebrew word used in Psalm 22:6 for "worm" is תּוֹלַעַת (tola'at). Literally, this word refers to a lowly worm or maggot— a small, crawling creature associated with decay and insignificance. In the context of the verse, it expresses the psalmist's extreme sense of humiliation and degradation:

"But I am a worm, and not a man, a reproach of men and despised by the people." (Psalm 22:6, NASB)

The psalmist does not merely feel small—he feels subhuman, scorned, and entirely devalued by others. The term "worm" is thus an intense image of vulnerability, contempt, and shame.

2. Symbolic and Theological Significance

Beyond its literal sense, tola'at carries symbolic weight, especially in theological interpretation. The word refers not just to any worm, but specifically to the crimson worm (Coccus ilicis), which was historically used to produce a red dye. This crimson dye was used in the tabernacle and priestly garments (see Exodus 26:1; 28:5), giving the word cultic significance in relation to sacrifice and holiness.

In Christian theology, the use of tola'at in Psalm 22 is often seen as a prophetic foreshadowing of Jesus Christ’s suffering and crucifixion. Psalm 22 is widely regarded as a Messianic psalm, and Jesus quotes from it on the cross (Matthew 27:46; Mark 15:34). In this interpretation, the "worm" symbolizes:

  • Jesus’ voluntary humility and degradation (Philippians 2:6–8)
  • The scorn and mockery He endured during the Passion (Matthew 27:29–31)
  • The shedding of His blood, represented by the red dye of the crimson worm

3. Biblical Cross-References

Other passages that echo or shed light on this concept include:

  • Isaiah 41:14 – "Do not fear, you worm Jacob, you men of Israel; I will help you," declares the Lord (YHWH – He is).
  • Job 25:6 – "How much less man, that maggot, and the son of man, that worm!"
  • Exodus 26:1 and 28:5 – References to the scarlet/crimson material (Hebrew: tola'at shani) used in priestly and tabernacle garments.
  • Philippians 2:6–8 – Speaks of Christ’s humility and death on a cross.
  • Matthew 27:46 and Mark 15:34 – Jesus cries out, quoting Psalm 22:1.

Thus, Psalm 22:6 uses the word "worm" not only as a vivid expression of human shame and suffering but also as a deeply meaningful image in redemptive history—ultimately pointing to the suffering and sacrificial role of the Messiah.
